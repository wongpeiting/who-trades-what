Did their trades beat the market?

Were these trades unusually well timed? For every stock and ETF trade we can price, we compare its return against the S&P 500 over the next 30 and 90 days, plus a short five-day window where trading on a coming event would show up. Buying just before a stock rises counts as good timing, and so does selling just before it falls and dodging the loss.

How to read this

This screen covers only the stocks and ETFs we could match to a ticker, so the municipal and corporate bonds that make up most of the President's 2026 trading fall outside of this analysis. Because filings report each trade as a dollar range rather than an exact figure, trades cannot be weighted by size.

For each trade we take the price from the first market close on or after the disclosed transaction date and subtract the S&P 500's move over the same window, so the trade is judged against the broader market rather than against the market's own swings. A purchase beats the market when the stock outruns the index; a sale beats it when the stock lags, meaning the filer sold ahead of a relative slide.
