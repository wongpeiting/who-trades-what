Trading what their agency funds

When an official who helps steer an agency's spending also trades the stock of a company that agency pays, their private finances and their public duties point the same way. A contract decision meant to serve the public can also lift the official's own holdings, and no one can prove which motive was at work. That is the conflict these trades expose, listed with how much the official's own agency has paid each company.

Traded near an agency action

An agency's actions, its rules, approvals and orders, can move the stocks it touches. So when an official trades a company just as their own agency acts on that industry, the timing raises questions: did they trade on what they knew was coming, or did an action they helped shape move a stock they held?

This screen flags every trade within seven days of a dated action by the official's own agency: Federal Register rules for Cabinet departments, and for the President his own tariff orders, all filed since Jan. 20, 2025. Note: The action may have nothing to do with the company traded.
