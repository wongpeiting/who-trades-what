Methodology

{# ── 3/4: the methodology write-up ── #}

This database brings together the financial disclosures of President Trump and 35 senior administration officials into a single, searchable dataset of more than 40,000 reported holdings and transactions.

Covering filings from Jan. 20, 2025, through a July 5, 2026 snapshot, it links each disclosure to market prices, federal contract awards and dated agency actions. That makes it possible to ask whether an official's trades beat the market, whether they traded in companies their own agency pays under contract, whether a trade came close to a decision that agency made, and whether it was reported on time.

How were the filings turned into searchable data?

Disclosures at the heart of this database are public, but locked inside thousands of pages of government PDFs — the most revealing of them scanned by hand into images that make it hard for computers to parse. Making them searchable took two tools working in tandem: optical character recognition (OCR) to lift text straight off the page, and a large language model to read the scans and garbled tables where OCR returned poor results.

natural-pdf handled the bulk of the extraction. It lifts embedded text straight off the page where the PDF carries a real text layer, and falls back to OCR (via easyocr) on the scanned and blurry filings. OCR output is noisy — garbled words, amounts split by stray spaces, dates that don't parse cleanly — so a set of normalizers repairs the known failure modes before any row is loaded. Dates required more care in particular, as a misread one would throw off both the late-filing and market-timing calculations that depend on it. The parser reads both four- and two-digit years, and every one of the 30,253 transaction dates resolves to a real date.

The President's eight 2026 periodic 278-Ts were the exception. Their scans were too degraded for OCR to recover cleanly, so those pages were handed to a vision-language model instead — a fan-out that read each page range the way a person would and returned structured rows, recovering close to 7,000 trades that had otherwise been unreadable. Because a model is a statistical reader, that transcription was validated rather than trusted blind, as detailed under How was the data checked below.

How were securities matched to tickers?

The table shows the security description as filed, but under the hood each one is matched to a stock ticker, because the ticker is the key the market-return, contract and agency-action analyses join on. Names are matched against SEC EDGAR with rapidfuzz, and a match requires a distinctive, non-generic shared word. This ensures that a common term like "Bancorp" or "International" cannot collapse many companies onto one ticker.

About 87% of trades resolve. The rest — municipal bonds, private funds and other instruments that have no exchange ticker — carry none, which is why the market-return analysis covers equities only.

Where does the data come from?

The filings are public records. Senior executive-branch officials must file them under the Ethics in Government Act of 1978; the U.S. Office of Government Ethics publishes them, and they are public domain (17 U.S.C. §105). Two forms make up the core:

  • OGE Form 278-T — a periodic report of each securities trade over $1,000, due within 45 days (required since the 2012 STOCK Act).
  • OGE Form 278e — an annual, or nominee, report of everything held and earned. President Trump's 2026 annual is a 927-page 278e covering his transactions, holdings and income.

Everything else is public reference data, used only to add context to what the officials disclosed: USASpending.gov for federal contract awards, SEC EDGAR for company tickers, the Federal Register for dated agency actions, and daily market prices from Yahoo Finance for returns. For the executives who previously served in Congress, the information around their prior office comes from the unitedstates/congress-legislators project.

What's included, and what isn't?

The database covers President Trump's second administration: the disclosures he and 35 of his senior officials — 34 Senate-confirmed Cabinet and agency heads, plus one nominee — have filed since the term began on Jan. 20, 2025. Anyone for whom a disclosure exists is included, captured in a snapshot taken on July 5, 2026. In all, the filings run to 41,719 disclosed lines: 30,253 transactions and 11,466 holdings, gathered into one searchable table.

The disclosure forms exempt several asset classes — among them the underlying holdings of excepted investment funds, mutual funds, Treasury securities, the Thrift Savings Plan and real property — so this database does not cover that information.

How was the data checked?

Every one of the 41,719 rows links back to the original filing PDF, so any figure can be traced to the page it came from. Those pages were also spot-checked: a language model pulled random rows and read them back against the source PDF, comparing the security, date, transaction type and amount line by line. In the most recent sample every verifiable row matched its filing — including the vision-transcribed trades, where the model had to tell near-duplicate lines apart, such as "3M CO" against a separate "3M COMPANY" entry.

The vision-model transcription was validated against a hand transcription of a small subset of rows, and every transcribed row is tagged extracted_by=vision_model so it can be re-audited.

87% of trades were matched to a ticker. This was done conservatively, with an ambiguous name left blank rather than force-matched to prevent wrong ticker matches from quietly corrupting downstream figures, including executives' market-return and contract analyses.

What are some known limitations?

The dollar figures are approximate by design. Filers report amounts as ranges rather than exact sums, apart from a few "net proceeds" income lines, and the forms exempt whole asset classes — the underlying holdings of excepted investment funds, mutual funds, Treasuries, the Thrift Savings Plan and real property — so the totals here understate each official's true wealth. Most holdings also come from nominee, pre-confirmation filings, and those portfolios may since have been divested as a condition of confirmation.

The flags in this database are best treated as leads to pursue, not conclusions to draw. A well-timed trade, or one placed near an agency action, is a starting point for reporting, not proof of wrongdoing. A trade may have been directed by a broker or adviser, the filer may not have chosen the date, and an agency action may have no bearing on the company traded.

The "industry they regulate" flag involves a judgment call. It is applied only to appointed officials with a defined portfolio, not to the President: he nominally oversees every industry, so flagging all of his roughly 28,000 trades would tag everything and reveal nothing. His potential conflicts surface through the other flags instead, such as tariff-pause trades, and trades in companies the Executive Office of the President pays under contract.

Reach out
